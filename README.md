# Leadership Tips — Building a High-Performing Team

A digest of team-leadership principles, plus communication tactics from **Jessica Chen** (Emmy-winning communication expert, author of *Smart, Not Loud*, Columbia University instructor).

---

## 1. Tips for building a high-performing team

1. **Be unbothered — calm is contagious.** Your team calibrates their stress to yours. When things break, respond with "okay, what do we know?" instead of panic. A steady leader gives the team permission to think clearly.
2. **Psychological safety — it's okay to fail and move.** Punish hiding problems, never revealing them. Run blameless postmortems: "what did the system allow?" not "who messed up?" Google's Project Aristotle found this is the #1 predictor of team performance.
3. **Trust = competence × character.** People trust you when you're both good at your job *and* consistent in how you treat them. Extend trust first — micromanaging tells people you expect them to fail.
4. **Ask, don't tell.** In 1-on-1s ask "what does a win look like for you?" and "what's getting in your way?" — then remove the blocker. Listening is the fastest trust-builder.
5. **Set the standard, not the method.** Be crystal clear on *what* great looks like and *why*, then let people own the *how*. Autonomy with clear goals is where high performance lives.
6. **Praise publicly, correct privately, be specific.** "Great job" is noise; "the way you handled that objection was excellent because..." teaches and motivates at once.
7. **Protect the team's focus.** Absorb organizational noise, unnecessary meetings, and shifting priorities so the team can do deep work.

## 2. Message to instill confidence in the team

> "I want you all to know something: I didn't build this team by accident. Every person here was chosen because you're capable of more than you probably give yourselves credit for. We're going to take on hard things, and sometimes we'll get it wrong — that's fine. Mistakes made while pushing forward are tuition, not failure, and I will never hold an honest attempt against you. What I care about is that we learn fast, back each other up, and never let a teammate struggle alone. You don't need to be the loudest person in the room to be valuable here — you need to be curious, honest, and willing to grow. I trust this team completely, and my job is to clear the path so you can prove me right. Let's go do work we're proud of."

## 3. Jessica Chen digest — *Smart, Not Loud*

Core idea: many of us were raised in a **Quiet Culture** (humility, deference, head-down hard work, harmony) but work in a **Loud Culture** workplace. You don't have to become loud to be seen — you have to communicate *smart*.

### The 4A Sequence (how to speak up in meetings)
1. **Active listening** — spot the right moment to chime in.
2. **Acknowledge** — credit the previous speaker first, so it never feels like you're cutting them off.
3. **Anchor** — pick up the last few words of the previous speaker and build on them; the conversation flows instead of clashing.
4. **Answer** — then make your point.

Bonus tactic: **speak within the first 5–10 minutes** of a meeting — the longer you wait, the more you'll talk yourself out of it.

### Trust & audience-first communication
- Before speaking, ask yourself: **"Who am I speaking with, and what do they care about?"**
- Build trust by asking *them* questions about what success looks like for them, instead of proving how smart you are.
- Her "golden question of communication": **"What's the point I'm trying to make here?"** — great to teach anyone who rambles.

### Quiet Capital Framework (getting noticed without being loud)
1. **Shape your career brand** — decide what you want to be known for.
2. **Establish credibility** — visible competence, delivered consistently.
3. **Advocate for yourself** — celebrate wins publicly, ask for what you want, and follow up confidently and repeatedly.

### Executive presence
- It's inner confidence plus making others feel **heard, valued, and engaged** — not a title.
- Stay focused in conversations; mirror body language (smile when they smile, nod when they nod) to show you're listening.

## 4. Topics to keep studying / impart to the team

**For the leader:** situational leadership, radical candor (care personally, challenge directly), delegation as development, managing energy not just time.

**For the team:** extreme ownership ("no one is coming — it's on us"), growth mindset, the 1% rule (small daily improvements compound), and speaking up smart (the 4A Sequence above).

**Books:** *Leaders Eat Last* (Sinek) · *Radical Candor* (Scott) · *Extreme Ownership* (Willink) · *Smart, Not Loud* (Chen).
